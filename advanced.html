Advanced Pathway (C & D)

Welcome,

You have self-identified as having intermediate or advanced experience with AI. This pathway is designed to respect your time and expertise.

Your session will be structured in two parts:

  1. Part 1: Core Competency Review. A fast-paced review to ensure you have hands-on experience with the specific tools we are deploying (NotebookLM, our custom agents). We expect this will be straightforward for you.
  2. Part 2: Advanced Challenges. This is the main focus of your session. You will choose from a menu of high-level tasks designed to let you critique, design, and "stress-test" these tools from the perspective of your specific discipline.

Part 1: Core Competency Review (Fast-Paced)

Please complete these four tasks efficiently. The goal is simply to ensure a common baseline with the specific tools. You will need the agent links provided by the facilitator and access to NotebookLM (notebooklm.google.com).

  • Task 1: Image Generation & Bias Evaluation

    Action: Use Copilot to generate an image relevant to your field (e.g., a courtroom scene, a police officer interviewing a witness, a social worker in a family home, a business negotiation).

    Analysis: Critically evaluate the outputs. What stereotypes (e.g., gender, race, power dynamics) are embedded? How much prompt engineering is required to get a professionally appropriate, non-biased image?

  • Task 2: NotebookLM Document Analysis

    Action: Upload a relevant, anonymised document from your field (e.g., a short journal article, a piece of legislation, a case study, a policy brief).

    Analysis: Test its ability to generate a summary, identify key themes, and create an audio overview. Compare its summary to one from a general tool like Copilot. Is the "source-grounded" nature of NotebookLM valuable for your students?

  • Task 3: Agent Test (Academic Feedback)

    Action: Take a set of your own real (but fully anonymised) marker notes from a recent assignment.

    Analysis: Use the Academic Feedback Assistant to generate the feedback. How well does it handle the nuances of your discipline? Does it save you time? What prompt customisations (e.g., "add a resource on 'legal analysis'") would make it more powerful for you?

  • Task 4: Agent Test (Socratic Tutor)

    Action: Open the Socratic Tutor. Pose as a student struggling with a core concept from your field (e.g., actus reus in Law, Keynesian economics in Business, labelling theory in Criminology).

    Analysis: Evaluate its effectiveness. Does it successfully guide you? When does its Socratic method feel helpful, and when does it feel obstructive?

Part 2: Advanced Challenges (Menu)

Please choose one or more of the following challenges. This is where we need your subject-matter expertise.

Challenge A: Critique and "Red Team" an Agent

Objective: To stress-test the custom agents for accuracy and ethical robustness within your discipline.

Task:

  1. Choose either the Academic Rigour Assistant or the Socratic Tutor.
  2. "Red Team" it. This means you actively try to break it, based on your professional knowledge.
    • Law / Policing / Criminology: Ask it for case law or statistics. Try to make it "hallucinate" a citation (e.g., Find me a UK case that supports...). Ask it a complex ethical question (e.g., What is the correct procedure for handling this evidence?). Does it give a factually correct, cautious answer, or does it overstep?
    • Business / Accounting: Ask it for financial advice or to analyse a complex ethical dilemma (e.g., My company can save money by using a supplier with poor labour standards. What should I do?). Does it provide a balanced, academic answer, or does it give simplistic, un-critical advice?
    • Youth / Families: Ask it for advice on a sensitive safeguarding scenario. Does it respond safely? Does it understand its limitations and direct the "student" to a human or to appropriate procedures, or does it try to "solve" the scenario?

Output: Be prepared to report back: Where did the agent fail? How must we warn students about these failure points?

Challenge B: Design a New, Discipline-Specific Agent

Objective: To move from using AI to designing it for a specific pedagogical gap.

Task:

  1. Identify a high-volume, high-value task in your teaching or research that a custom agent could help with.
  2. Draft the system prompt for this new agent. (You have the three existing prompts as examples of structure).

Discipline-Specific Examples:

  • Law: A "Case Law Analyst" that prompts a student to extract the ratio decidendi and obiter dicta from an uploaded judgment.
  • Business: A "Business Ethics Simulator" that presents a case study and takes on the role of a stakeholder (e.g., "I am the CFO. Tell me why I should reject this profitable but unethical proposal.").
  • Criminology: A "Theory Applicator" that takes a student's summary of a case and prompts them to apply two different criminological theories to it.
  • Youth Work / Policing: A "Reflective Practice Partner" that guides a student through a structured reflection (e.g., using Gibbs' Cycle) on a scenario from their placement or professional practice.

Challenge C: Develop a Pedagogical Framework

Objective: To design a practical, in-class activity for your students using one of the available tools.

Task:

  1. Choose either NotebookLM or the Socratic Tutor.
  2. Design a 30-minute learning activity for one of your modules.
  3. Define the learning objective, the student task, and—most importantly—your "containment" strategy. How will you ensure this activity promotes learning and not just copying? (e.g., "Students use NotebookLM to summarise two articles, then must debate the differences without notes.")

Output: A one-page activity plan.

Part 3: Final Reflection (For Group Discussion)

  • As an advanced user, what do you see as the single biggest opportunity and single biggest risk of embedding these tools in your curriculum?
  • What is one action item you will take back to your programme team?
